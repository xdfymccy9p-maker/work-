1. Equal Pay: Fairness or Unrealistic Expectation?

Essay
0 words
Full Essay

In debates about women and work, equal pay is usually presented as a simple moral equation: if two people do the same job, they should receive the same paycheck. At first glance this sounds obvious and almost boring, but once we look at how workplaces actually operate, equal pay turns into a complex mix of fairness, market logic, and social expectations. Some employers defend pay gaps by saying that salaries are just a result of experience, negotiation skills, or “the market,” while many women highlight concrete examples where they are clearly paid less than male colleagues for doing very similar work. The question, then, is not only whether equal pay is fair, but whether it is realistic in today’s labour market and what changes would be needed to get closer to it.

One important argument for equal pay is that it protects the basic idea of dignity at work. When a woman and a man sit in the same office, with the same job title and performance, but one receives a noticeably smaller salary, the message is that one person’s work is worth less. This does not just affect the individual employee; it shapes how society values women’s time, education, and contribution to the economy. Research from organisations such as the International Labour Organization and the OECD shows that even after controlling for education and experience, women in many countries still earn less on average than men. This suggests that the gap is not only about personal choices, but also about structural patterns in hiring, promotion, and pay.

However, critics argue that equal pay rules can be unrealistic or even unfair if they ignore legitimate differences between workers. For example, two people with the same job title might have different levels of responsibility, different sales results, or different working hours. Companies also worry that strict regulation will reduce their flexibility to reward top performers or to react quickly to market conditions. Some employers use this argument to resist pay transparency laws or salary audits. The problem is that “flexibility” can easily become a convenient cover for biased decisions that are never questioned, because nobody can see the numbers.

A more realistic way to understand equal pay is not as a promise that every salary will be identical, but as a commitment that gender itself will not be the reason for the difference. To achieve this, workplaces need tools like pay transparency, clear salary ranges for each position, and regular equal-pay audits carried out by independent teams. When employees are allowed to discuss their salaries without fear, hidden injustices become visible. Several European countries that introduced these measures did not suddenly erase every pay gap, but they were able to reduce unexplained differences and force companies to justify their pay structures using objective criteria instead of vague explanations about “fit” or “market value.”

In conclusion, equal pay is both a fairness issue and a realistic goal—if we define it carefully and support it with strong policies. It is unrealistic to expect a world in which every worker in the same job earns exactly the same amount, but it is completely reasonable to demand that gender should never be the silent factor that pushes a woman’s salary down. When governments require transparency and organisations take responsibility for monitoring their own pay systems, equal pay stops being a slogan on social media and becomes a concrete standard for evaluating whether a workplace is truly just.

2. Remote Work: Freedom or Distraction?

Essay
0 words
Full Essay

The rapid growth of remote work during and after the COVID-19 pandemic has changed what we imagine when we hear the word “office.” For many women, especially those balancing paid work with unpaid care responsibilities, remote work feels like long-awaited freedom: no commute, more control over the schedule, and the possibility of staying in the labour market while also taking care of children or elderly parents. At the same time, teachers, managers, and even some employees worry that working from home can reduce focus, weaken team spirit, and turn the home into a place of constant distraction. The reality lies somewhere in between these two extremes, and it depends heavily on how remote work is designed and supported.

On the positive side, remote work can open doors that were previously locked. Women who live far from major cities, who have limited access to transportation, or who face social restrictions on late-night commuting suddenly have access to global jobs through a laptop and a stable internet connection. Employers also benefit from a wider talent pool and can hire people based on skills rather than physical presence in a specific building. Studies by organisations such as McKinsey and the World Economic Forum indicate that flexible and remote work arrangements are strongly linked to higher retention rates for women, particularly after major life events like childbirth. In this sense, remote work can be a practical tool for reducing the gender gap in workforce participation.

At the same time, the “freedom” of remote work can quickly turn into invisible pressure. Without clear boundaries, some women find themselves answering emails late at night, taking calls during family time, and feeling guilty whenever they are away from the screen. Instead of having two separate spaces—home and office—they end up with a single blended environment where they are expected to be available for everyone at once. This can increase stress and make it hard to fully concentrate on either work or personal life. In addition, not every home is a quiet, private, or safe place for productive work, and not every worker has access to reliable technology and high-speed internet.

Another concern is that remote workers, especially women, can become “out of sight, out of mind” when it comes to promotions, networking, and leadership opportunities. Informal conversations that happen in physical offices—over coffee, in hallways, or during quick team meetings—often play a big role in how managers form opinions about who is committed and who is ready for more responsibility. If women are more likely to choose remote or hybrid options, they risk being seen as less dedicated, even if they deliver the same or better results. Without intentional policies, remote work can unintentionally reproduce old inequalities in a new digital form.

For remote work to function as true freedom rather than a new type of distraction, organisations need to create clear structures. This includes setting reasonable expectations for availability, providing equipment and technical support, training managers in leading hybrid teams, and measuring performance based on results instead of physical presence. Workers, on their side, need support in learning how to design routines, protect focus time, and communicate boundaries to both colleagues and family members. When these elements are in place, remote work can offer women more control over their careers and their daily lives, without forcing them to choose between productivity and wellbeing.

In short, remote work is not automatically liberating and not automatically harmful. It is a tool whose impact depends on power, policy, and culture. Treated thoughtfully, it can expand women’s options and make the workplace more inclusive. Treated carelessly, it can deepen exhaustion and hide inequality behind the comfortable image of working in pajamas. The challenge for employers, policymakers, and workers is to shape remote work in a way that keeps its benefits—flexibility and access—while actively reducing its risks, especially the risks that fall most heavily on women.

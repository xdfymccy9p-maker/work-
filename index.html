1. Equal Pay for Equal Work

Fair wages and basic justice

Why pay equity is more than a slogan.

“Equal pay for equal work” sounds like a simple rule: if two people do the same job with the same level of responsibility, they should receive the same salary, regardless of gender. In practice, however, closing pay gaps remains difficult in many labour markets (International Labour Organization, 2022). Even when formal discrimination is illegal, women still tend to earn less on average than men across their working lives (World Economic Forum, 2024).

Official statistics from international organisations show that wage differences are rarely the result of one manager openly deciding to pay women less (International Labour Organization, 2022; OECD, 2017). Instead, they emerge from a mixture of biased hiring decisions, unequal access to promotion, and the historical undervaluation of occupations where women form the majority of workers. These structural factors mean that pay gaps can persist even when individuals believe they are being fair in everyday decisions.

From a legal perspective, equal pay has already been recognised in many international agreements. For example, the International Labour Organization and the European Union require equal pay for equal work or for work of equal value, which means that jobs with similar skill, effort and responsibility should not be paid differently simply because one field is dominated by men (International Labour Organization, 2022). The World Economic Forum’s global index still shows slow progress in closing the economic participation gap between women and men (World Economic Forum, 2024).

Critics sometimes argue that it is hard to implement equal pay rules in practice. Salaries depend on market conditions, negotiation, and individual performance, and employers may worry that detailed pay audits will be costly or may create conflict. Yet countries that require regular reporting have shown that it is possible to identify “unexplained” gaps and reduce them gradually over time (OECD, 2017; World Economic Forum, 2024). In this sense, pay transparency becomes a tool not only for fairness but also for building trust.

Equal pay is not only a women’s issue; it affects the overall legitimacy of the workplace. When employees believe that their efforts are valued fairly, they are more likely to stay, invest in their skills, and recommend the organisation to others. For students preparing to enter the labour market, understanding how pay systems work—including rights to information and protection from discrimination—is a basic part of career planning (Dashti et al., 2024). Closing wage gaps is therefore both a moral obligation and a long-term investment in a more productive, stable work environment.

2. Remote Work: Freedom or Distraction?

Flexibility and hidden pressure

How working from home changes women’s experience of work.

Remote work expanded rapidly during the COVID-19 pandemic and has now become a permanent option in many sectors (Bloom, 2021; CQ Researcher, 2023). Supporters often emphasise flexibility: skipping the commute, organising the day more freely, and combining paid work with family responsibilities. For many women who carry a large share of childcare and housework, this flexibility can be the difference between staying in the labour market and leaving it (International Labour Organization, 2023).

At the same time, remote work blurs the borders between professional and private life. Instead of leaving the office at the end of the day, workers stay connected to their laptop or phone. Messages arrive at night, and online meetings can fill almost every hour. Studies show that long or irregular working hours harm work–life balance and are associated with lower life satisfaction (International Labour Organization, 2023; World Economic Forum, 2023). For women, this can turn into a “double shift” of paid work and unpaid care.

Employers worry that remote workers may be less productive or less committed, but evidence is mixed. Some research finds that productivity can remain stable or even rise when goals are clear and tools are reliable (Bloom, 2021). The real risk is often invisibility: remote workers may receive less mentoring and fewer informal networking opportunities, which are crucial for promotion to leadership roles (LeanIn.Org & McKinsey & Company, 2024).

For this reason, the debate should not be reduced to a simple choice between “remote” and “office.” Hybrid models—where employees spend part of the week at home and part on site—are increasingly presented as the most realistic solution (CQ Researcher, 2023). Clear rules about working hours, response times, and meeting schedules can protect private time while still allowing flexibility.

For students and young professionals, learning to manage digital communication, set boundaries and ask for written expectations is now a key employability skill (Dashti et al., 2024).

Pro notes – Remote work

  • Discussion starter: “Does remote work reduce or increase gender inequality in promotion opportunities?”
  • For your presentation, contrast one advantage (flexibility) with one risk (invisibility) and connect both to real data.

3. Women in Leadership: Quotas or Merit?

Representation and power

Rethinking how leadership positions are filled.

In many organisations, leadership is still dominated by men even when women are well represented among students and junior employees (LeanIn.Org & McKinsey & Company, 2024). Supporters of gender quotas argue that talent alone is not enough to guarantee that women will reach decision-making positions because selection processes are often influenced by stereotypes and informal networks (England, 2010; OECD, 2017). From this perspective, quotas are a temporary tool to correct structural barriers.

Critics worry that quotas could damage the perceived legitimacy of women who enter leadership roles under such policies. They argue that positions should be filled “purely on merit,” and that numerical targets are unfair (England, 2010). However, this assumes that existing systems already reward merit in a neutral way.

Evidence from corporate and political life suggests otherwise: when recruitment and promotion are informal, small biases repeated over time can produce leadership teams that look very different from the wider workforce (LeanIn.Org & McKinsey & Company, 2024; World Economic Forum, 2024).

A balanced view recognises that quotas are not a magic solution but can be effective when combined with clear criteria and transparent procedures. For example, specifying the skills and responsibilities required for each leadership role, using diverse selection panels, and documenting decisions all help ensure that any candidate—male or female—is chosen for solid reasons. Meanwhile, mentoring programmes and leadership training for women create a stronger pipeline of candidates (Dashti et al., 2024).

Ultimately, the debate is not just about numbers; it is about what kind of leadership organisations value. Research shows that more diverse leadership teams are associated with higher profitability and better decision-making (LeanIn.Org & McKinsey & Company, 2024; OECD, 2017). For students, this debate is also personal: it shapes how they imagine their own future roles—as engineers, managers, or researchers—and whether they feel invited into positions of responsibility.

  • Question for the audience: “Would you support a 40% target for women on company boards in your country? Why or why not?”

4. Overtime Culture: Dedication or Exploitation?

Long hours and health

Long working hours and their impact on women.

In some workplaces, staying late is treated as a sign of loyalty and professionalism. Over time, occasional overtime becomes the new normal, and leaving on time is interpreted as a lack of commitment (International Labour Organization, 2023). This culture ignores a basic fact: productivity is not the same as the number of hours spent in the office or online.

Research on working time shows that very long hours are linked to higher risks of burnout, mental-health problems and physical illness (International Labour Organization, 2023; World Economic Forum, 2023). For women, the effects can be even heavier because many still carry the main responsibility for unpaid care work. When they return home after a long day, the “second shift” of household tasks begins.

These pressures influence career decisions. Some women may refuse promotions that would require constant overtime, shift to part-time roles, or leave the labour market entirely (OECD, 2017). From a business perspective, this means losing experienced workers and weakening the talent pipeline. It also sends a message to younger employees that “success” is only possible for those who can ignore family or personal responsibilities.

A more sustainable approach focuses on realistic workloads and clear priorities instead of heroic stories about never-ending work. Companies can limit expected hours, discourage unnecessary late-night emails, and design hybrid schedules that still respect free time (International Labour Organization, 2023). For students, this discussion is relevant to both future jobs and current study habits: learning how to protect sleep, health and relationships is part of long-term professional success (Dashti et al., 2024).

  • Class activity: ask classmates to map a “24-hour day” for a man and a woman in a dual-earner household and compare hidden labour.
  • Useful concept: “second shift” to describe unpaid work after paid work.

5. The Gender Pay Gap: Myth or Reality?

Evidence and interpretation

Understanding an ongoing public and academic debate.

The term “gender pay gap” appears frequently in media and political debates, but people do not always agree on what it means or why it exists. Usually, the gap is calculated as the difference between the median hourly wage of men and women across the whole economy (World Economic Forum, 2024). On its own, this figure does not explain the reasons behind the gap, but it does show that men and women often end up in different parts of the wage distribution (OECD, 2017).

Critics argue that once education, work experience, occupation and hours worked are taken into account, the remaining difference becomes much smaller. They conclude that the pay gap mainly reflects personal choices—such as women choosing part-time work or lower-paid fields (England, 2010). However, this argument leaves open an important question: why are these “choices” more common for women, and how are they shaped by childcare systems, workplace cultures and social expectations?

A growing body of research treats the gender pay gap as the combined outcome of several forces: unequal division of unpaid work, limited access to affordable childcare, occupational segregation, and subtle bias in hiring and promotion (England, 2010; OECD, 2017). From this perspective, the gap is neither a pure myth nor a fixed law of nature. It is a pattern that changes when laws, policies and organisational practices change.

For students and young professionals, the debate is not only academic. Knowing how salary negotiations work, understanding rights to equal pay, and choosing employers that take transparency seriously can all make a concrete difference over the course of a career (Dashti et al., 2024). The aim is not simply to “win” an argument about whether the gap exists, but to build labour markets in which pay reflects skills, effort and responsibility more than gender.
